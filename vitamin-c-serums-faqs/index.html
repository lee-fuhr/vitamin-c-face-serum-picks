Vitamin C Serums questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Vitamin C serums are potent antioxidants that brighten skin, fade dark spots, boost collagen, and protect against environmental damage. For best results, choose a stable form like L-ascorbic acid at 10–20% concentration, apply it in the morning under sunscreen, and store it in a cool, dark place to prevent oxidation.

What does vitamin C serum do for your skin?

Vitamin C is a heavy-hitting antioxidant. When applied topically, it neutralizes free radicals from UV exposure and pollution, which helps slow premature aging. It also interferes with melanin production, gradually lightening hyperpigmentation and giving your complexion a more even, radiant look. Beyond brightening, vitamin C is essential for collagen synthesis. Regular use can improve skin firmness and reduce the appearance of fine lines. Many dermatologists consider it a cornerstone of a daytime routine, especially when paired with sunscreen – the combination offers more protection than sunscreen alone.

Which form of vitamin C should you look for?

The most researched and potent form is L-ascorbic acid. It works fast but is finicky – it requires a low pH (around 3.5 or below) to penetrate, and it’s prone to oxidation when exposed to air, light, or heat. Look for opaque, airless pumps and dark glass bottles to protect it. Derivatives like ascorbyl glucoside, ethyl ascorbic acid, and tetrahexyldecyl ascorbate are gentler and more stable. They’re ideal for sensitive skin or if you don’t want to worry about a short shelf life. However, they typically need to convert in the skin to become active, so results may take a bit longer. There’s no single “best” – it depends on your skin’s tolerance and your storage habits.

What concentration is right for you?

For most people, a concentration between 10% and 20% L-ascorbic acid offers the best balance of efficacy and tolerability. Starting at 10% is wise if you have sensitive skin or are new to actives. Higher strengths (15% or 20%) can cause stinging or irritation, especially on compromised barriers, but they deliver faster results in fading spots and boosting collagen. If you’re using a gentler derivative, concentrations can range higher without irritation – some go up to 20% or more – because the delivery is slower. Pay more attention to the ingredient’s position in the ingredient list and the brand’s reputation than to the exact percentage label.

How should you layer vitamin C in your routine?

Vitamin C works best on clean, dry skin. After washing your face in the morning, apply a few drops of serum before any other products. Wait about 60 seconds for it to absorb, then follow with moisturizer and, crucially, broad-spectrum sunscreen. This morning order protects the serum from oxidizing and ensures it penetrates evenly. At night, vitamin C can still be used, but it’s less common because you lose the photoprotective synergy with sunscreen. If you do use it at night, apply it after cleansing but before heavier treatments like retinol. Avoid combining L-ascorbic acid directly with niacinamide – layering them separately (one morning, one night) is perfectly fine and avoids any potential pH mismatch.

How can you tell when your vitamin C serum has gone bad?

A fresh L-ascorbic acid serum is typically clear or pale yellow. As it oxidizes, it turns a deeper yellow, then brown. Once it’s dark brown or amber, it has lost most of its efficacy and can even irritate the skin. Discard it immediately. Derivatives tend to stay clear longer, but they, too, can degrade – watch for changes in color or a sour, metallic smell. To extend the life of any vitamin C serum, store it in a cool, dark spot – not in a bathroom where humidity fluctuates. Refrigerating it can help, but avoid freezing. An airless pump bottle is far better than a dropper because it limits air exposure each time you use it. Most undiluted L-ascorbic acid serums have a shelf life of about three to six months once opened.

How long until you see results from vitamin C?

Vitamin C is not a quick fix. Most people notice a brighter, more even complexion after about four to eight weeks of daily use. Fading dark spots or sun damage can take three to six months, and collagen-related improvements – like fewer fine lines – may require consistent use for six months or longer. Consistency matters far more than concentration. One drop a day every day will outperform a high-strength serum used sporadically. And because vitamin C’s antioxidant benefit is immediate (it quenches free radicals as soon as it’s applied), you’re still getting protection even before you see visible changes.

Frequently asked questions

Can vitamin C cause purging?

Vitamin C is not a chemical exfoliant, so it typically does not cause purging. If you break out after starting a new serum, it may be a reaction to other ingredients or a sign of irritation from a too-high concentration. Try reducing frequency or switching to a gentler derivative.

Is it okay to use vitamin C if you have oily or acne-prone skin?

Absolutely. Vitamin C can help reduce inflammation and post-acne marks. Look for an oil-free, lightweight formula labeled “non-comedogenic.” L-ascorbic acid can feel sticky on oily skin at high concentrations, so a fermented or derivative version may feel more comfortable.

Should you use vitamin C in the morning or at night?

Morning is ideal because vitamin C boosts the protective power of sunscreen and scavenges free radicals from UV and pollution throughout the day. Night use is still effective, but you miss that synergy. If your skin tolerates it, morning application is the most researched approach.

Can you mix vitamin C with retinol?

It’s generally fine to use both in your routine, but not in the same layer. The best practice is to apply vitamin C in the morning and retinol at night. If you want to use them at the same time, apply them on separate days or wait at least 30 minutes between layers to avoid unnecessary irritation.

Why does my vitamin C serum sting or tingle?

A mild tingling can be normal, especially with L-ascorbic acid at a low pH. But sharp stinging or burning usually means the formula is too acidic for your skin, or it has started to oxidize. Try a lower concentration, a buffered derivative, or check that the serum hasn’t turned brown. If it persists, discontinue use.

Do you need to refrigerate vitamin C serum?

Not strictly, but it can help extend the life of a short-lived L-ascorbic acid serum. Keep it in a cool, dark place away from humidity. Refrigeration adds a few extra weeks before oxidation. Avoid leaving it in a hot car or direct sunlight.

In shortThe best vitamin C serum is the one you’ll use consistently. Start with a stable, well-reviewed formula at a strength that suits your skin type, apply it in the morning, and always follow with sunscreen. Patience pays off – within a few months you should see brighter, more even skin. And when in doubt, a patch test is never a bad idea.
